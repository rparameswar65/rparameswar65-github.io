## Learning Portfolio: Emerging Technology Research

## Article Selected: *The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis*

**Source:** Citrini Research, authored by James Van Geelen and Alap Shah  
**Published:** February 2026  
**Link:** [https://www.citriniresearch.com/p/2028gic](https://www.citriniresearch.com/p/2028gic)

---

### (a) What is this article about?

*The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis* is a widely discussed scenario analysis
published by Citrini Research in February 2026. Written as a fictional macro memo
from June 2028, it explores a single high-stakes question: *What if AI adoption
succeeds beyond all expectations — and that very success triggers a catastrophic
economic collapse?*

The authors frame this not as a prediction, but as a **left-tail risk scenario** —
a low-probability but high-impact outcome that markets and policymakers are not
adequately preparing for.

---

### (b) What are the main arguments?

The article identifies a chain of interconnected risks it calls the
**"Intelligence Displacement Spiral"**:

1. **Reflexive AI Adoption** — Businesses facing competitive pressure are forced
   to adopt AI agents at scale, replacing white-collar roles in software
   engineering, finance, legal, and middle management.

2. **Ghost GDP** — Productivity gains flow primarily to the owners of compute
   infrastructure (e.g., Nvidia, cloud providers), not to workers or consumers.
   The economy appears healthy on paper, but consumer spending erodes.

3. **SaaS / Private Credit Contagion** — As AI agents allow companies to build
   software in-house, subscription-based software companies (SaaS) lose recurring
   revenue. This triggers defaults in private equity-backed debt held by major
   asset managers.

4. **Prime Mortgage Fragility** — Unlike 2008, the mortgage crisis in this
   scenario hits *high earners* — tech workers in San Francisco, Seattle, and
   Austin — as structural unemployment strips them of income and they begin
   defaulting on the $13 trillion residential mortgage market.

5. **Policy Gap** — Traditional tools like interest rate cuts cannot address
   large-scale labor displacement. The article envisions a political stalemate
   around proposed solutions such as AI compute taxes and a "Shared AI Prosperity
   Act" (public dividends funded by AI-generated output).

By mid-2028 in this scenario: unemployment exceeds 10%, the S&P 500 falls 38%
from its 2026 peak, and an "Occupy Silicon Valley" protest movement blocks
AI company offices for weeks.

---

### (c) What type of AI is involved?

The article focuses on **Agentic AI** — autonomous AI systems capable of
completing multi-step tasks without human supervision. By 2027 in the scenario,
AI agents:

- Write all software code
- Handle weeks-long research projects autonomously
- Optimize personal and corporate spending

This represents a shift from AI as a *tool* (assisting humans) to AI as an
*agent* (replacing human cognitive labor independently).

---

### (d) How does this affect everyday life?

The scenario projects several real-world consequences:

- Entry-level white-collar jobs disappear first, breaking the career pipeline
  for new graduates
- Displaced workers move into lower-paying roles or trades, depressing wages broadly
- Households drain savings and turn to credit cards to make mortgage payments
- New AI-era jobs (prompt engineers, AI safety researchers, infrastructure
  technicians) pay a fraction of the jobs they replace
- Social unrest grows as inequality between compute-owners and displaced workers widens

---

### (e) Is this article credible? What are the counterarguments?

The article sparked significant debate. Citadel Securities released a formal
rebuttal arguing that Citrini makes the same analytical error as John Maynard
Keynes, who in 1930 predicted automation would eliminate most human work.
Instead, productivity gains historically lowered costs, created new industries,
and expanded consumption — generating new jobs even as old ones disappeared.

Critics also note that AI adoption faces real-world constraints including
chip availability, data center construction timelines, energy supply, and
regulation — making a rapid two-year collapse unlikely.

A balanced review (Medium, 2026) found the **entry-level job displacement
concern to be the most validated**: the share of junior-level workers at major
tech companies was cut in half between 2023 and 2025, and entry-level job
postings dropped significantly. Senior roles that require judgment and strategy,
however, currently show AI complementarity rather than substitution.

## Conclusion

*The 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis* is a provocative and well-constructed
stress test of AI's economic risks. While experts debate whether the catastrophic
scenario is realistic, the underlying concerns — labor displacement, income
concentration, and policy gaps — are already visible in today's data. Whether
or not the worst outcomes materialize, the article is a valuable framework for
thinking critically about AI's societal impact beyond productivity metrics.
